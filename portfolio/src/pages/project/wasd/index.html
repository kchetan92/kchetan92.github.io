WASD: The Indoor Navigation Solution

Problem Statement

The navigation interface for vehicle doesn't extend very well for pedestrians in a multistory building.

GPS navigation has become a common place thanks to Google maps. It has allowed us to explore new territories without the fear of getting lost. However, navigation for pedestrians is a different ball game.

My Role

Researcher, UX Designer (solo project)

UX Skills

Sketching, storyboarding, wireframing, prototyping, user testing

Platform

Mobile

Duration

4 months (Fall 2017)

Tools

Adobe Illustrator, InVision

Goals

Rethink navigation for pedestrians inside a building. Identify opportunities and pain points.

Research

Aim: Understand what a user goes through when navigating a building. How do people remember and recall a 3D structure.

Methodology: I interviewed some of my batchmates to find out about their experiences in navigating new building. I wanted to know what helps them search and recall their way through the structure.

Since people rely on intuition to find their way through a building, I couldn't get a complete picture through interviews alone.

I used a form of cultural probe to identify what people remember about a building. I asked them to draw a map of a floor which the frequent often.

Result: From the interviews and probe, I learnt that

  • Users remember which corridor and side their room of interest is, but do not remember how many doors/windows are there in the corridor.

  • Users often fail to remember the rooms they don't frequent. One of the female participants, couldn't recall where the mens' washroom was.

  • None of the users indicated the actual distance they walk (in ft or meters) to arrive at their destination.

Personas

Persona helps me empathize with the users. I created these personas from my user research and referred to them often later in the process.

Experience map

Finding your way around a building is a complicated procedure, and can become an extended ordeal for the user. This experience map provides a way to track users thoughts, actions and feelings. This is very helpful when deciding on the content and layout of various screens of the interface.

Competitors

Google maps is one of the most popular apps for vehicle navigation. Combined with the vast amount of data that Google has, they're your safest bet when exploring an unknown territory. The indoor maps feature in Google maps now features major structures such as airports and malls.

GPS technology is not very reliable indoors. Companies like insoft, accuware, NavVis, IndoorAtlas, use WiFi positions, bluetooth beacons or vision based navigation to get the location.

Inference: While there are multiple tech companies focusing on indoor navigation, there aren't many which focus on the UX of indoor navigation. Some malls/airports have their own dedivated app to help in navigation but they're rarely used.

Storyboarding

The next step towards identifying the solution is sketching out some possible ideas. I sketched around 8 different solutions (diverge) before I could arrive (converge) at one idea that I would be working on.

Low-fi Prototype

The groundwork setup till this stage is used to create the low fidelity prototype. Reasonable rough sketches of the final interface were made with well developed interaction strategy. The main purpose of the low-fi prototype is to quickly generate some sketches so that it can be tested with some users.

Usability Testing

Testing the prototype with a volunteers revealed that

  • Users didn't recognize some icons. Better icons or helper text might help.

  • Some of the initial loading screens were confusing. The user didn't know how to proceed. Most of the loading can be done in the background.
