Brushy Buddy

Ubiquitous Design to promote Dental Hygeine among kids

GALE, Summer ’18

Overview

This was my first ubiquitous computing project. Good user research practices can be applied to many areas and the end result need not be a mobile or a desktop interface. Brushybuddy is a tool aimed at young children to teach them good brushing behavior. The challenge was to design a tool which was easy to use and wouldn’t obstruct the regular brushing process and still be enjoyable, motivating and easy to understand.

Ubiquitous computing strives to keep the human computer interaction to a minimum. An ideal ubiquitous computing system blends into the environment and uses the users’ natural movement as input. We incorporated sensors in the toothbrush, and used the feedback from the movement of the toothbrush to drive the system. This was easy for any child to operate.

background

Motivation

Dental healthcare awareness among young children is a major among the parents. During a recent casual conversation among parents about their kids, I heard statements like:

Secondary research revealed that 5-years-old only brush 25% of the surface of their teeth, compared to 50% of 11-years-old and 67% of 18-22-years-old (Rugg-Gunn & Mactretor, 1978).

My group and I concurred that this is a challenging problem statement and we started with some exploratory interviews to get a better sense of the problem.

observation: video diary study

Understanding the end user

To understand the children’s behavior towards brushing, we conducted video diary studies. Parents were asked to record a video of their children at home brushing their teeth. We found that

Children feel excited about the process: such as filling the cup with tap water, squeezing toothpaste from tube and stirring water to wash the brush.

Children could not brush properly: some kids suck toothpaste off the brush, some brush teeth horizontally and press hard. Parents are concerned about these habits.

Children could not brush thoroughly: usually neglected areas include biting surfaces of the back teeth and back of the front teeth

Converge #1

Early sketches

Based on our findings, we sketched some initial ideas to address this issue.

survey

Validating findings

The concepts developed in the previous step were too broad. Based on feedback from peers, we decided to conduct additional research to narrow our scope. A survey was conducted and data from 55 responses were analyzed. The findings validated the interview findings.

user enactments

Putting the concepts to test

Based on survey, interview and video diary study we realized that the following factors can affect the design of the product

Involvement of parents. We want to know how the participation of parents affect the children and parent’s peace of mind.

Story, music or game. Different means can be adopted to develop interest in the rather monotonous activity of brushing. This is necessary to prolong the brushing activity to 2 minutes.

Device type: toothbrush / mirror / toothbrush holder. The team presented different arguments for and against each type of device.

We created a matrix to select from the combination of all the above factors and selected 5 prototypes to test with the participants. We made paper prototypes and prepares scripts for this. Following questions can help us judge the success of the chosen prototype:

How might we encourage children to brush teeth for enough time?

How might we help children to brush teeth thoroughly?

How might we help parents to supervise children’s tooth brushing behavior?

Storytelling

The user was introduced to the story and was shown a picture of the part of the mouth to brush. As they brushed, the progress bar was moved to show the completion percentage. After each segment, the user was shown the next part of the story and a new region in the mouth to brush their teeth. The story was divided into 6 segments to cover all the parts of the mouth.

Toothbrush Holder

The scenario involves one participant in the role of kid without parents’ presence. It is now in the morning and the kid go to the bathroom to brush his/her teeth. As the kid picks up the toothbrush from the holder, the holder will start to interact with the kid to help the kid go through the toothbrushing process.

Musical animated character

The toothbrush holder plays teethbrushing music with the kid’s name, to remind the user that it is time to brush teeth. The cartoon character gives verbal intruction of how to brush teeth, by singing “left, left, right, right, circle, circle, one more time…”. At the end of th two minutes, the toothbrush holder will stop the music and give the kid a sticker as little prize.

Toothbrush Holder Game

The kid will have a tooth-brushing competition with parent/sibling. The bars on the mirror shows the competition status. The lights on the toothbrush holders indicate the areas the kids need to brush. There’s a sensor in the toothbrush that can sense if the kid is brushing in the right way (circle/direction: up and down/) and if the kids fully cleaned this area. If you are doing wrong, the toothbrush holder will remind the kid to brush the teeth correctly and the competitor will exceed the kid.

Insights from parents

Parents feel that they must teach kids how to brush their teeth. Therefore they do not want a tool to replace their tutoring function entirely. However, parents do hope that some tool could be in place to help kids brush their teeth correctly when they are busy or not in a good mood (e.g., in a busy morning).

Insights from Kids

Kids (3-5) cannot be pushed too hard in learning the correct tooth brushing behavior. Based on their ability as a very young kid, they cannot do the brushing movements very agilely, and we cannot ask them for 100% correct. It would discourage kids, and make them frustrated. It would be better to focus on the long-term goal by teaching the kid gradually and step by step.

Insights about device

It will be distracting if we use mirror and toothbrush holder at the same time to show visual instructions (like the game prototype we did) since kids need to look up to see the mirror and then look down to see the toothbrush. Displaying visual instructions on the mirror is the most natural way to incorporate the smart device into the toothbrushing experience.

physical prototype

Building the smart mirror and toothbrush

Based on our learnings from the user enactment, we concluded that we should not take the responsibility of teaching kids how to brush from their parents. The device should only promote, verify and report/record the brushing behavior. We also noticed that the toothbrush should not be too distracting; otherwise children will lose interest from the brushing exercise.

We propose to solve this issue by using interactions in the smart mirror. The interactions on the mirror will not distract the user from brushing. It will also monitor their progress and motivate them with fun visualization of their brushing progress. (i.e. the brushed areas turning from yellow to white, and the artificial germs disappear gradually.)

The parent will be able to monitor the children’s progress by using a companion app. With video tutorials, the app is able to show users how to brush in a correct way. From our research, we found that more often than not, parents are not 100% sure about the brushing skills. In this case, the app could serve as a good helper to parents.

The toothbrush assembly consists of a gyroscope and accelerometer sensor. The yaw, pith and roll value of the toothbrush is gathered from this sensor as the user brush their teeth. One of the limitations of using this approach is that we have to ask the user to hold the toothbrush still in front of their mouth to allow the sensor to calibrate the starting position. The subsequent positions calculated are relative to the initial position. The sensor data is sent to a server via serial communication over a bluetooth module.

We use Origami Studio to build the display for the mirror that gets the data from the server where we upload the data from the sensor. In Origami, we make the screen display the representative visuals that we made with Google Poly 3D models by translating the integer data into the corresponding area of the mouth. During the tooth brushing process, the screen display gets data from the server every second and changes the visual to display the specific area as the angle of the toothbrush changes.
