Water Connection Transition

PROBLEM

Communication gap between the tenants, owners and the Water Department

Every year thousands of student move in and out of the City of Ann Arbor, a college town home to the University of Michigan. Many students opt to rent Individual houses in Ann Arbor across the city. To offload the responsibility of paying water bills and dealing with overdue bills, the owners ask the tenants to take the ownership of the water connection. Which seems reasonable but miscommunication ensues. Tenants end up paying fines and have to wait for the water connection to be activated.

Currently, every time a tenant vacates his house he calls the city to terminate his water connection. If the owner doesn't call to take the responsibility of the water connection, it is manually shut down by the Response Team, and a charge of $26 is levied on both the owner and the tenant. The water is turned on manually again when a new tenant arrives, and the fine is levied again.

Stakeholders

BACKGROUND RESEARCH

Other cities have better proesses

We looked at how other college towns handle this process and found that none of them have such complicated procedure to shift water connections. Some cities did not allow transferring water connection to tenants, while some transferred the water connection to the owner without shutting them off. However, merely adopting policies from other cities is not a feasible option. Such changes, known as "policy changes" can be passed after the city council approves it. It is a lengthy process.

Technology to the rescue?

Electronically operated valves are well developed and are used in many cities around the world. However, they're expensive to install and maintain. We wanted to propose a solution which could be implemented easily.

PROCESS

Interview protocol for contextual inquiry interviews

From our initial interview with the client, we learned of the various people involved in the process. Based on their role, we prepared an interview protocol to extract the necessary details.

Interviews

We interviewed the stakeholder in groups of two, where one was designated as the interviewer and the other as the note taker. We looked for visual cues such as their work environment and body language and used techniques like 5 Whys questions to extract information.

Affinity Mapping

The affinity notes were transcribed from the recordings of the interview and peer-reviewed. We then grouped them on the affinity wall to find the prominent themes of the project.

Ideas

After hours of comprehensive interviewing and brainstorming we presented four recommendation to the client. The recommendations were easy to implement and the client appreciated them. Details of the recommendations can be provieded on request.

Learning

This project was an incredible learning experience. When reading about the research process, I found it hard to believe that simple technique such as five whys, affinity notes or affinity wall can give any useful insights. But the process helped us decipher and arrange the data efficiently.
